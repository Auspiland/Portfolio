# The STAR Method: A Guide for Resumes (Cover Letters) and Interviews

## 1. What Is the STAR Method?

STAR is an experience-based structuring framework widely used in interviews and cover letters (자소서). It organizes an experience into a logical flow by describing: in what **Situation (S)** you faced, what **Task (T)** you were assigned, what **Action (A)** you took, and what **Result (R)** you achieved.

**Core principle:** The goal is not merely to state *what you did*, but to clearly show *why*, *how*, and *what outcome it led to*. This makes it highly effective for conveying key experiences persuasively.

### The Four Components

| Component | Meaning | Key Question | Suggested Length |
|-----------|---------|--------------|------------------|
| **S (Situation)** | The background/context of the experience; the problem situation | When, where, and in what context did it occur? | 2 sentences |
| **T (Task)** | The role you held; the problem or goal you had to solve | What was my role, and what did I need to solve? | 1–2 sentences |
| **A (Action)** | The actions you executed; the effort and process | What specific actions did I take? | 4–5 sentences |
| **R (Result)** | The outcome, achievement, and lessons learned | What were the results, and what did I learn? | 3 sentences |

This framework transforms a flat, list-like cover letter into a logical, structured story, and helps produce writing that is easy for an interviewer to read.

> **TIP:** The guiding rule is *"Keep S and T short; make A and R specific."* What an interviewer wants to know most is **"What did you learn, and how would you improve it if you did it again?"**

## 2. How to Write Each Section

### S — Situation: When, Where, and in What Context?

This step briefly explains the starting point. Concisely and clearly describe the applicant's environment, role within the organization, and the background at the time. Overly detailed background disrupts the flow, so summarize in roughly **2–3 lines**.

**Example:**
> "In my third year of university, I participated in an on-campus advertising competition for the first time. The team was composed of various roles, including planning, design, and video production."

**Writing tips:**
- Choose experiences based on *what you learned* rather than just *the achievement*.
- Prefer experiences that reveal a problem-solving process over simple awards or projects.
- Examples: resolving conflicting team opinions, improving marketing data analysis, getting a service-planning idea adopted.

---

### T — Task: What Did You Need to Do?

Explain what problem you needed to solve or what goal you were assigned. Don't just list your role; clearly state the essence of your responsibility and the task to be solved. Specifying your distinct role within the team conveys a proactive image.

**Example:**
> "I was in charge of the team's content strategy planning, taking on the task of improving the reach of our SNS (social media) campaign."

**Writing tips:**
- Present *"the role I took on"* together with *"the specific problem/goal."*
- If you have numbers or KPIs, present them clearly as the task (e.g., doubling SNS reach, increasing visitors by 30%).

---

### A — Action: What Did You Do, and How?

**This is the most important part of the cover letter.** Describe, with a focus on the concrete process, what you actually did to solve the task mentioned in the previous step. Rather than vaguely saying "I worked hard," describe the judgments you made, the methods you used, how you collaborated, and what tools you applied.

**Example:**
> "To define the target audience, I planned an online survey with my teammates and analyzed hashtag responses based on the collected data. I then used A/B testing to design a content flow centered on the most effective material."

**Writing tips:**
- Make *"what I did"* clearly visible.
- For collaborative work, emphasize *"the part I led."*
- It is fine to organize this in 2–3 lines per stage (planning / execution / adjustment).
- Suggested action-description structure: **my perception → analysis/tool use → team collaboration → execution result.**

---

### R — Result: What Changed?

The final step summarizes the result of your actions and what you learned. Where possible, focus on numbers, metrics, and achievements—but even without quantifiable results, clearly state what you learned and how you grew.

**Example:**
> "Campaign reach increased by 180% compared to the previous average, and the team won an award of excellence in the competition. Through this experience, I felt firsthand how effective a data-driven content strategy can be, and I have since approached projects with analysis at the center of my thinking."

**Writing tips:**
- Include concrete results where possible: numbers, achievements, awards.
- Even if the results are modest, emphasize lessons learned, attitude changes, or relevance to the target job.
- Close with the message: *"This experience connected to my competencies."*

## 3. Structured Template Example

```
[S] During university, I participated in an on-campus advertising competition.
[T] As the team's strategy planner, I was responsible for the content structure
    and target analysis needed to increase the campaign's effectiveness.
[A] I conducted a target-audience survey and A/B-tested content types based on
    SNS hashtag response data. As a result, I designed a content flow that
    prioritized high-reach material.
[R] As a result, campaign reach improved by over 180% versus the average, and
    I internalized the importance of designing content based on data.
```

---

## Quick Reference Summary

- **STAR = Situation → Task → Action → Result.**
- **Length balance:** S and T short; A and R detailed.
- **A (Action) is the most important section** and should be the most concrete.
- **R (Result)** should favor quantified outcomes, but lessons learned are a valid substitute.
- **Experience selection:** prioritize a visible problem-solving process over a prestigious-sounding outcome.
